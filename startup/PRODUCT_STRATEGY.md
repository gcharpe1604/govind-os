# Product Strategy

> **Purpose:** Make directional decisions — what to build, what to cut, when to pivot, and how to think about long-term product direction.

---

## What Product Strategy Is

Product strategy is the set of decisions that determine:

- Which problem you are solving.
- For whom.
- Why your approach wins against alternatives.
- What you will not do.

Strategy without tradeoffs is not strategy. It is a wish list.

---

## Strategy Mindset

Good product strategy requires saying no more than yes.

- Every yes to a feature is a no to something else.
- Every yes to a user segment is a de-prioritization of others.
- Clarity about what you are not building is as important as what you are building.

*"Strategy is choosing what not to do." — Michael Porter*

---

## The Strategy Stack

```text
Problem → User → Insight → Differentiation → Roadmap → Metrics
```

Each layer must be grounded in evidence from the layer below it.

---

## Positioning

Define your position clearly:

> "We help [specific user] do [specific job] better than [alternative] because [specific reason]."

If you cannot complete this sentence clearly, your strategy is not clear enough.

---

## Prioritization

Use these questions to prioritize decisions:

1. Does this help us test our core hypothesis?
2. Does this create meaningful value for our target user?
3. Does this move our key metric?
4. Does this differentiate us from alternatives?
5. Is this the highest-leverage thing we can do right now?

If a feature or decision does not clearly answer yes to at least two of these, deprioritize it.

---

## Metrics That Matter

Measure metrics that reveal real user behavior.

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Activation | User completed the core action for the first time |
| Retention | User returned after day 1, day 7, day 30 |
| Engagement | Frequency of core action per user |
| Revenue | Paid conversion, ARPU |
| Referral | Organic growth, NPS |

Avoid vanity metrics: total signups, page views, social followers.

*The metric that matters most depends on your current hypothesis.*

---

## Pivot vs. Persist

When results do not match expectations, decide:

**Persist when:**
- The hypothesis is right but execution is weak.
- You have not yet reached enough users to get a valid signal.
- The problem is validated but the solution needs iteration.

**Pivot when:**
- Multiple iterations have failed to produce behavior change.
- User evidence consistently points to a different problem or user.
- The market is too small or too resistant to support the current direction.

A pivot is a structured change of direction, not an abandonment of all learning.

---

## Pivot Types

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| Problem pivot | The problem you solved is not the right one |
| Segment pivot | Different users need this more than your original target |
| Solution pivot | Same problem, different solution approach |
| Business model pivot | Same product, different revenue mechanism |
| Channel pivot | Same product, different distribution method |

---

## Roadmap Principles

- A roadmap should reflect current learning, not permanent plans.
- Roadmaps should be revisited after each significant learning cycle.
- Near-term items should be specific. Long-term items should be directional.

Avoid committing to long-term features before validating near-term hypotheses.

---

## Common Strategy Failures

- **Building for the average user:** Average users often have average pain. Target extreme users.
- **Strategy by committee:** Consensus produces incremental thinking, not differentiated products.
- **Premature scaling:** Scaling a product that has not found product-market fit amplifies problems.
- **Ignoring retention:** Acquisition without retention is a leaking bucket.
- **Competitor obsession:** Building in reaction to competitors instead of in response to user needs.
