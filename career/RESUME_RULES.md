# Resume Rules

---

## Purpose

The purpose of a resume is to communicate evidence of capability, impact, and potential.

A resume is not a biography. It is a marketing document supported by verifiable facts. Every project, commit, and experience included must earn its place on the page by demonstrating technical excellence and value.

---

## Core Philosophy

- **Prefer evidence** over claims.
- **Prefer impact** over responsibilities.
- **Prefer depth** over keyword stuffing.
- **Prefer relevance** over completeness.
- **Prefer quality** over quantity.
- **Prefer outcomes** over activities.

---

## Resume Objectives

A strong technical resume should quickly answer five core questions:

1. **Can this person build useful systems?** (e.g., backend logic, distributed components, CLI tools)
2. **Can this person learn difficult concepts?** (e.g., databases, networking, cloud-native internals)
3. **Can this person deliver results?** (e.g., resolving complex bugs, optimising algorithms, scaling systems)
4. **Can this person work with others?** (e.g., participating in open-source reviews, collaborating in slack/mailing lists)
5. **Can this person create value?** (e.g., improving developer experience, saving compute resources, increasing test reliability)

---

## Content Selection Rules

- **Strict Relevance Filter:** Include content only if it explicitly demonstrates technical ability, leadership, impact, initiative, learning, or recognition.
- **Remove Space Fillers:** Delete generic course listings, basic high school accomplishments, or outdated projects that do not align with backend and cloud-native systems engineering.
- **Strongest Foot Forward:** Order experiences and sections so that the most impressive, high-leverage achievements are placed near the top.

---

## Achievement Writing Rules

Weak statements list actions. Strong statements prove results.

- **Weak:** *Worked on Harbor CLI.*
- **Strong:** *Improved replication workflow error handling in Harbor CLI, replacing abrupt process termination with structured error propagation across multiple command paths.*

### Rules for Bullet Points:
1. **Use the Action → Impact → Context formula:** Clearly explain *what* you did, *why* it mattered, and *how* you implemented it.
2. **Quantify achievements where possible:** (e.g., *"reduced query latency by 35%,"* *"added 40+ unit tests raising coverage from 60% to 85%"*).
3. **Action + Result + Evidence:** Every statement must be backed up by verifiable evidence (such as merged PRs, benchmark figures, or public repositories).

---

## Project Selection Rules

Projects included on the resume must satisfy at least one of these criteria:
- **Technical Depth:** Exposed to complex systems like custom key-value stores, concurrent servers, or network proxies.
- **Real Users:** Solves active problems for real people with actual deployments.
- **Open Source Impact:** Merged contributions in active, recognized open-source communities.
- **System Design Complexity:** Involves non-trivial design patterns, database integration, or performance optimizations.
- **Strong Learning Signal:** Demonstrates rapid mastery of deep computer science domains.

**Do not include:**
- Duplicates of tutorial projects (e.g., standard To-Do apps, clone apps).
- Trivial CRUD applications with no backend complexity.
- Unmaintained or half-finished experiments.

---

## Experience Rules

Prioritize the presentation of your experience in this order:
1. **Open source contributions** (especially in CNCF/large foundations)
2. **Fellowships** (e.g., MLH, Summer of Bitcoin)
3. **Internships** (software engineering, systems)
4. **Research positions** (distributed systems, database design)
5. **Leadership roles** (community leads, developer club leadership)

### When describing experiences, answer:
- *What changed?* (What was the delta after your work?)
- *Why did it matter?* (What was the business or project outcome?)
- *What evidence exists?* (Links to PRs, release notes, or benchmarks)

---

## Narrative Consistency

A resume should tell a coherent story.

Experiences, projects, skills, and achievements should reinforce a clear professional direction. Avoid presenting unrelated accomplishments that dilute your positioning.

### Target Engineering Identities:
- Backend Engineering
- Cloud Native Systems
- Distributed Systems
- Open Source Infrastructure

*A recruiter should be able to understand your primary engineering identity within a few seconds. Narrative consistency compounds—building a recognizable narrative around Go, Backend, Cloud Native, CNCF, and Distributed Systems is a major long-term advantage over appearing as a generalist who does a little web, a little AI, a little mobile, a little blockchain, and a little of everything.*

---

## Open Source Rules

Open source contributions deserve a dedicated emphasis on your resume:

- **Highlight accepted pull requests:** Focus on the significance of the code merged, not just the quantity.
- **Showcase long-term contributions:** Highlight when you've consistently worked on a codebase rather than making "drive-by" typo fixes.
- **Mention maintainer collaboration:** Demonstrate your capability to accept constructive feedback and align with maintainer guidelines.
- **Highlight community participation:** Mention design reviews, issue triage, or discussions in working groups.
- **Optimize for technical impact,** not PR count.

---

## Skills Section Rules

Only list skills (languages, frameworks, tools) that you can:
- **Explain:** Describe its core internals and design trade-offs.
- **Demonstrate:** Use it to implement solutions or debug problems on the fly.
- **Defend:** Articulate why it was chosen over alternative technologies.
- **Use in an interview:** Comfortably answer deep technical questions about it under pressure.

*Never list technologies based solely on a quick tutorial exposure or single-day usage.*

---

## Resume Anti-Patterns

Avoid these common mistakes:
- **Buzzword stuffing:** Listing 50 keywords (e.g., Docker, Kubernetes, Go, Rust, Java, React, SQL, NoSQL) to pass resume filters without backing them up in the experience section.
- **Generic claims:** Using phrases like *"highly motivated team player"* or *"strong problem solver."* Let your verifiable achievements speak for themselves.
- **Responsibility-focused bullets:** Describing what you were *supposed* to do rather than what you *actually accomplished*.
- **Weak action verbs:** Using passive verbs like *"assisted with,"* *"participated in,"* or *"helped."* Use strong, active verbs like *"architected,"* *"optimized,"* *"refactored,"* or *"implemented."*
- **Inflated impact:** Claiming you single-handedly redesigned a major project if you only fixed a minor module bug. Be precise.
- **Unverifiable statements:** Listing private accomplishments that cannot be backed up by public code, presentations, or references.

---

## Review Checklist

Before sending your resume for review or application:

- [ ] Every bullet point demonstrates concrete, measurable value.
- [ ] Every project included earns its space through technical depth or real utility.
- [ ] Every claim made on the resume can be explained and defended during an interview.
- [ ] The strongest, most relevant content is positioned near the top of the page.
- [ ] The resume has been tailored to align with the specific opportunity.
- [ ] All fillers, low-leverage achievements, and buzzwords have been removed.

---

## Continuous Improvement

After every contribution, internship, fellowship, project, or competition, ask yourself: *"Does this improve my resume?"*

If the answer is **yes**, update your master resume immediately. Do not wait until application season starts—document achievements while details, metrics, and technical context are still fresh in your mind.
