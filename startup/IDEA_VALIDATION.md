# Idea Validation

> **Purpose:** Determine whether a problem is real, painful, and worth solving before writing a single line of code.

---

## What Validation Means

Validation is not asking people if they like your idea.

Validation is finding evidence that:

- A specific problem exists.
- People experience it frequently and painfully enough to seek solutions.
- The problem is not already solved well enough.

*Absence of complaints is not the same as absence of problems.*

---

## Validation Mindset

You are not trying to confirm your idea. You are trying to kill it cheaply.

- Seek disconfirming evidence actively.
- Treat weak validation as no validation.
- A problem is real when people describe it without being prompted.

---

## The Problem-First Framework

```text
Problem → Who Has It → How Painful → How Often → Current Solutions → Gap
```

Answer each stage before moving forward. Skipping stages produces false confidence.

---

## Step 1: Define The Problem Hypothesis

Write one sentence:

> "I believe [specific person] experiences [specific problem] when [specific situation]."

Vague problem hypotheses produce vague validation.

---

## Step 2: Identify The Target User

Define:

- Who experiences this problem most acutely?
- Are they a specific role, demographic, or context?
- Can you reach them for a conversation?

Avoid defining the user as "everyone." The narrower the target, the clearer the signal.

---

## Step 3: Customer Discovery Conversations

Goal: Understand the problem, not pitch the solution.

**Before the conversation:**
- Prepare open-ended questions.
- Do not describe your idea.
- Do not ask hypothetical questions ("Would you use...").

**During the conversation, ask:**
- "Tell me about the last time you experienced X."
- "What did you do when that happened?"
- "What tools or workarounds do you use today?"
- "What's the most frustrating part of your current process?"

**Strong validation signals:**
- They describe the problem without prompting.
- They have already tried to solve it with makeshift solutions.
- They express frustration with existing alternatives.
- They ask when your solution will be ready.

**Weak or false validation signals:**
- "That sounds interesting."
- "I might use that."
- "Yeah, that could be useful."

---

## Step 4: Market Sizing

Validate that the problem affects enough people to build a business.

| Level | Question |
|---|---|
| TAM | How many people globally experience this problem? |
| SAM | How many can you realistically reach? |
| SOM | How many can you capture in year one? |

Do not obsess over precise numbers. Validate order of magnitude.

A small but intensely painful problem in a reachable market beats a large indifferent market.

---

## Step 5: Existing Solutions Analysis

- What are people using today to solve this problem?
- Why is it insufficient?
- What would they need to switch?

If existing solutions are genuinely good enough, the problem may not need a new solution.

---

## Validation Checklist

Before proceeding to MVP:

- [ ] Problem is defined in one sentence.
- [ ] At least 5 target users interviewed.
- [ ] Users described the problem unprompted.
- [ ] Users have attempted existing workarounds.
- [ ] Market size is large enough to justify building.
- [ ] No existing solution solves it well enough.

---

## Common Failure Modes

- **Solution-first thinking:** Building before validating the problem.
- **Survey bias:** Surveys produce polite answers, not honest ones. Use conversations.
- **Friendly feedback:** Friends and family will validate almost anything.
- **Hypothetical commitment:** "I would pay for that" ≠ "I will pay for that."
- **Skipping disconfirmation:** Only seeking evidence that confirms the idea.
