# Cover Letter Guide & Template

> [!NOTE]
> **Purpose of a Cover Letter:**
> A cover letter exists to explain why a specific opportunity and a specific candidate are a strong match. The goal is not to repeat the resume; the goal is to connect relevant experience, demonstrated capability, and organizational needs into a compelling narrative supported by evidence.

---

## Core Philosophy
- **Prefer evidence over enthusiasm:** Show what you have built or fixed rather than just saying how excited you are.
- **Prefer alignment over self-promotion:** Focus on how your skills meet the organization's requirements, not just your personal achievements.
- **Prefer impact over responsibilities:** Highlight the results of your actions, not just the tasks assigned to you.
- **Prefer specificity over generic statements:** Avoid generic templates; tailor every line to the target project.
- **Prefer demonstrated capability over potential capability:** Back your claims with merged PRs, active code links, or technical writeups.
- **Respect reviewer time:** Keep it concise, focused, and under one page.

---

## Evidence Hierarchy
*Not all evidence carries equal weight. Prioritize higher-signal evidence to demonstrate your capabilities.*

1. **Merged PRs in target repository:** Direct proof of capability and familiarity with the codebase.
2. **Merged PRs in related repositories:** Proof of ability to contribute to similar projects/technologies.
3. **Relevant side projects:** Production-grade codebases built from scratch.
4. **Technical writeups:** Demonstration of deep understanding, debugging process, and engineering logic.
5. **Certifications:** Least signal, but verifies foundational theoretical knowledge.

*Prefer higher-signal evidence whenever possible.*

---

## Cover Letter Formula
A successful cover letter progresses logically:

```
Opportunity  ⟶  Alignment  ⟶  Evidence  ⟶  Future Contribution
```

*Avoid the traditional structure of: Introduction ⟶ Life Story ⟶ Conclusion.*

---

## Before Writing
Verify your research before drafting:
- [ ] Have I thoroughly researched the organization and its repository?
- [ ] Have I read and understood the specific project description and scope?
- [ ] Do I know what evaluation criteria the mentors/reviewers prioritize?
- [ ] Have I identified my most relevant experience or contributions?
- [ ] Have I prepared the strongest evidence (GitHub links, docs, blogs)?
- [ ] Is the content tailored exclusively to this opportunity?

---

## Structure

### 1. Opening
*State the opportunity, why it interests you, and why you are a strong fit.*

#### Template
```markdown
I am applying for the [Opportunity Name / Project Name] position at [Organization].

My background in [Relevant Area/Skill], combined with my work on [Specific Project/Contribution], aligns closely with the goals of this opportunity.

I am particularly interested in [Specific Aspect/Technology of the Project] because [Reason].
```

---

### 2. Alignment Section
*Explain why this specific opportunity, organization, and project is the right fit.*

#### Template
```markdown
I am particularly interested in this project because [Specific Reason, e.g., Harbor's evolution toward registry spec alignment or the CLI UX goals].

My previous experience with [Relevant Technology/Contribution/Concept] has given me exposure to many of the challenges involved in this area, such as [Specific challenge, e.g., concurrent connection handling or parsing Cobra configs].
```

---

### 3. Evidence Section
*The most critical part of your letter. Use the pattern:*

```
Experience  ⟶  Impact  ⟶  Evidence
```

#### Template
```markdown
Relevant examples of my experience include:

- **[Contribution / PR Link]:** Implemented X feature which solved Y problem, improving performance/stability by Z.
- **[Project / Repository Link]:** Built Y system to solve Z, demonstrating competency in [Specific Technology/Pattern].
- **[Open Source Work / Code link]:** Contributed to [Project], resolving [Issue] by rewriting/optimizing [Specific Area].

These experiences helped me develop and demonstrate skills in:
- [Skill X, e.g., idiomatic Go concurrency]
- [Skill Y, e.g., mock testing registry APIs]
- [Skill Z, e.g., writing CLI Cobra structures]
```

---

### 4. Contribution Section
*Explain what value you can create for the project if selected.*

#### Template
```markdown
If selected, I hope to contribute by:
- Improving [Specific component or area]
- Extending [Specific feature or subcommand]
- Supporting [Testing, docs, or community triage]

while continuing to learn from the maintainers and the broader community.
```

---

### 5. Closing

#### Template
```markdown
Thank you for considering my application.

I appreciate the opportunity to apply and would be excited to contribute to [Organization/Project].

Sincerely,

[Name]
```

## Specialized Variants
*Depending on the opportunity type, adapt your cover letter structure as follows:*

### 1. Open Source Cover Letter Variant (LFX, GSoC, SoB)
- **Project Interest:** Why this specific repo/feature?
- **Relevant Contributions:** Merged code, triaged issues, or documentation in the target repository.
- **Technical Alignment:** Proof of matching tech stack (e.g., Go, Docker, OCI).
- **Evidence of Execution:** Links to code, PRs, and active writeups.
- **Expected Contribution:** What will be delivered by the midterm and final milestones.
- **Closing**

### 2. Internship Variant
- **Company Interest:** Why this company's engineering team/culture?
- **Relevant Experience:** Past work or projects matching the team's stack.
- **Projects:** Systems built from scratch.
- **Open Source Work:** Upstream contributions demonstrating collaboration skills.
- **Future Contribution:** How you plan to hit the ground running.
- **Closing**

### 3. Research Program Variant
- **Research Interest:** The specific academic or industrial research topic.
- **Relevant Technical Background:** Mathematics, algorithms, or systems foundations.
- **Projects:** Implementations, benchmarks, or simulations built.
- **Technical Curiosity:** Unanswered questions in the domain you want to explore.
- **Future Goals:** Academic or long-term career aspirations.
- **Closing**

## Common Failure Modes
- **Repeating the resume:** Merely listing job histories and degrees without connecting them to the project.
- **Generic enthusiasm:** Stating "I am very passionate about this project" without showing evidence of that passion through code.
- **No evidence:** Claiming skill in a framework or language but providing no links to public code or contributions.
- **No organization research:** Sending a cover letter that doesn't mention the organization's specific goals or current technical stack.
- **Long personal stories:** Including irrelevant details about childhood, hobbies, or life histories.
- **Excessive flattery:** Over-praising the organization or mentors instead of focusing on mutual technical alignment.
- **One cover letter for all:** Using the exact same letter for multiple application channels.
- **Transactional focus:** Focusing entirely on how the opportunity benefits *you*, rather than how you can create value for *them*.

---

## Review Checklist
Run this check before submitting:
- [ ] Is it tailored exclusively to this specific opportunity?
- [ ] Is every claim of capability backed by a link to evidence (code/writeup/PR)?
- [ ] Have I referenced a specific sub-project, issue, or feature?
- [ ] Does it avoid repeating the chronological history in the resume?
- [ ] Is there a clear, realistic contribution narrative?
- [ ] Is the tone professional, objective, and evidence-based?
- [ ] Does it fit cleanly under one page?

---

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Opportunity-Specific Rule:** For LFX, GSoC, Summer of Bitcoin, and open-source opportunities, the strongest cover letters are often those with the least emphasis on generic motivation and the most emphasis on evidence. Reviewers trust contributions, technical understanding, and execution history far more than claims of passion.
